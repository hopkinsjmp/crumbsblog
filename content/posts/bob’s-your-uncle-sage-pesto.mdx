  *40g pine nuts

  *Salt and pepper

method: |-
  1. On a medium heat, lightly toast the pine nuts in a dry pan until golden brown. Keep an eye on them so they don’t burn.
  2. To a blender (feel free to use a freestanding or hand-held blender or, alternatively, a food processor—see intro) add the sage (leaves only), minced garlic clove, grated parmesan, oil, salt and pepper.
  3. Once the pine nuts are toasted, add these to the blender too. Now blend until it reaches your desired consistency (I make mine quite smooth).
  4. Add a spritz of lemon and blend once more. Now check for seasoning and whether it needs, for instance, a little more parmesan, garlic or olive oil.
  5. And… Bob’s your uncle!

I’ve had a love affair with sage ever since I learnt how well it went, fried, atop eggs (fried or scrambled for preference). You can use either olive oil or butter for this mission, making sure there’s enough to properly coat the sage leaves, turning them over once they start to look a mottled grayish-brown. Subsequently transfer onto some kitchen paper to drain and as it cools it crisps up so that, if one isn’t careful, it shatters in the process of choreographing the sage on top of the eggs. Compromised or not, it is THE perfect accompaniment to an egg brunch. But, be warned, too much and you’ll have something that tastes like cheap Sunday Roast stuffing.

Recently, I’d been playing around with the idea of a sage pesto so that I could get my sage fix in a different medium and on my time—alongside many other ingredients, my local supermarket is pretty unreliable at stocking it. A pesto seemed like a perfect—and easy—solution. So here you go. I don’t think the flavours are ground-breaking and I wouldn’t go too hard with the sage (see previous paragraph) but it does provide an accent of this green goodness which adds that extra _je ne sais quoi_ to any meal you deem worthy of it.

My suggestions: drizzle over a root mash (pictured)—I tend to go with a combination of sweet potato, carrot and parsnip; spread thickly over toast before topping with a fried egg; drizzle over tortellini or ravioli (pumpkin, cheese, or ricotta and spinach would be my recommendations); spread onto roasted chicken, pre or post cooking; add it to more olive oil or melted butter and anoint your vegetables with it; or, quite simply, add to some pasta for a quick dinner.

## A few notes on the recipe 

As with pretty much any recipe I post on this blog, it really is up to you to make the pesto as you like it. I like mine quite smooth so I blend it first in a food processor and then in a blender—a completely unnecessary two-step process. If you feel your blender is robust enough, just chuck everything in straight away. If you want a more ‘rustic’ grainy pesto, stick to a food processor or only blend for a few seconds. You could also try using a pestle and mortar, but I’ve not tested this myself.

It’s a little on the runny side, which I like, as it makes for excellent drizzling. However, if that’s not your bag and you want more of a paste, feel free to use less oil or pop it in the fridge so it firms up.
