Perhaps the title should be: Going Out on a Limb with Za’atar. Because I’m not sure where I’m going with this one. 

Last week, the Ball and Chain (AKA husband) came home and I shared with him the recent opinion pieces I had written for this blog, especially those focused on single food items (see my thoughts—or womansplaining—on cream, matcha, and scrambled eggs).

After which, the conversation continued along these lines:

Ball and Chain: “You should do one on Za’atar.” 

Me: “Why would I do one on Za’atar?”

Ball and Chain: “Because Za’atar always reminds me of you.”

Me: “Why on earth would Za’atar remind you of me?”

Ball and Chain: “Because you were the first one to introduce it to me, in one of the first meals you cooked for us.”

Me: “Yeah, but I don’t know anything about Za’atar and I don’t use it that often.”

Ball and Chain: “Well I don’t know, it was just an idea.”

One’s immediate response to this dialogue might be: Jesus guys, if these are the exciting conversations you’re having after 1.5 years of marriage then I don’t think this relationship has legs. 

Ok, note taken. Someone else, however, might generously and encouragingly agree with my husband and say: “Yes, you go girl, you write about an ingredient you have very little knowledge of, both regarding its origins and usage”.

Oh, alright then. I was first introduced to this majestic ingredient by one of Ottolenghi’s cookbooks. I believe it was _Simple_\*. I liked the sound of it, not just because it promised to inject any meal with flavours reminiscent of balmy middle eastern dusks, but because it basically took all the guesswork out of seasoning. Essentially being a combination of different herbs and spices—perfectly balanced—all I had to worry about was tasting for salt. 

Za’atar is a combination of oregano, marjoram, thyme, cumin, coriander, sesame seeds, salt and most importantly, sumac. And yes, I did just have to look that up. It’s not that I prefer ignorance, it’s that there’s something magical about the mystery of this ingredient. It just makes everything I use it with taste… well, better. It’s like having a cheat code for creating dishes perfect for winter nights and summer feasts: it seems to make both hearty food heartier and light food taste like a summer holiday. 

My combinations of choice? I like to chuck it on winter veg before roasting; coating it on a whole cauliflower before putting it in the oven—low and slow; sprinkling it on tahini when serving as a drizzle; adding atop shakshuka or natural yoghurt already laced with browned butter. I imagine it would go beautifully with eggs too—fried or poached—mashed potatoes, and polenta. It’s also perfect for hummus, after dotting over some olive oil. 

Like I said, I don’t quite know where I’m going with this beyond wanting to urge you to try it. Since Covid, I noticed it starting gracing the shelves of supermarkets and fancy specialist food stores. Of course, cheaper options are available at ethnic shops. In other words, there’s no excuse not to give it a go. 

Just think of it like the lazy way out of producing flavourful food. Or think of me because, apparently, that association has already been made, and fondly so. At least, the B&C doesn’t regret it.

And as you might have guessed: sales pitches were never my thing.

\*Ottolenghi, Yotam, _Simple_ (London: Ebury Press, 2018).
